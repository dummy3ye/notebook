The 2013 El Reno tornado is remembered not just for its record-breaking width, but for the loss of three of the most respected figures in the storm-chasing community. The **TWISTEX** (Tactical Weather-Instrumented Sampling in/near Tornadoes EXperiment) team was led by Tim Samaras, a man whose life's work was dedicated to understanding the internal dynamics of the world's most violent storms.

## The Team

*   **Tim Samaras (55):** A self-taught engineer and scientist. He was known for his calm, methodical approach to chasing. He held the world record for the largest pressure drop ever recorded inside a tornado.
*   **Paul Samaras (24):** Tim's son, a talented videographer who captured the raw beauty and power of the atmosphere.
*   **Carl Young (45):** A meteorologist and Tim’s longtime chase partner, whose expertise helped guide their tactical deployments.

## The Incident: May 31, 2013

On that fateful evening, the TWISTEX team was traveling in a white Chevrolet Cobalt on Reuter Road, attempting to place instruments in the path of a developing tornado near El Reno, Oklahoma.

The storm, however, was unlike anything they had encountered. It was rain-wrapped and difficult to see, and it underwent an "explosive" expansion, growing from less than a mile wide to over 2.6 miles in seconds. A violent sub-vortex—a smaller, faster-spinning vortex within the main tornado—struck their vehicle.

> "They were pioneers who sacrificed everything to help us understand the atmosphere."

## Legacy of Safety

The deaths of the TWISTEX team sent shockwaves through the meteorological world. It prompted a profound re-evaluation of safety protocols and the ethics of storm chasing. Today, the "Tim Samaras" name is synonymous with both scientific rigor and the inherent dangers of fieldwork.
