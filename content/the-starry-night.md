Vincent van Gogh’s *The Starry Night* (1889) is perhaps the most recognized work in Western art. Painted during his stay at the Saint-Paul-de-Mausole asylum in Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, it is a testament to the artist's ability to find infinite beauty in the midst of profound personal struggle.

## Composition and Energy

The painting is dominated by a turbulent, swirling sky—a sea of deep blues and violets punctuated by eleven pulsating stars and a glowing crescent moon.

### The Cypress Tree
In the foreground, a dark, flame-like cypress tree reaches toward the sky. Traditionally a symbol of mourning and death, the tree acts as a bridge between the quiet, terrestrial village at the bottom and the vast, celestial realm above.

## The "Impasto" Technique

Van Gogh used thick, short, rhythmic brushstrokes, a technique known as **impasto**. This gives the painting a physical texture and a sense of constant movement, as if the night itself is vibrating with energy. 

> "I have a terrible need of—shall I say the word?—religion. Then I go out at night to paint the stars."

## Astronomical Reality

While the central swirl is often seen as a reflection of Van Gogh's "turbulent" mind, some researchers believe it may have been inspired by sketches of spiral nebulae that were being published at the time. Despite its dreamlike quality, the painting was a highly disciplined effort, created from memory during the day based on observations made from his window at night.
