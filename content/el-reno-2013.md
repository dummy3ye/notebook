The May 31, 2013, El Reno tornado is a landmark event in meteorological history. It holds the record for the **widest tornado ever documented** and was the scene of the first known fatalities of scientific storm researchers in the field.

## Record-Breaking Dimensions

As the storm moved across the rural landscape near El Reno, Oklahoma, it reached a staggering peak width of **2.6 miles (4.2 km)**. 

### Intensity and Controversy
While mobile radar units (RaXPol) measured extreme wind speeds of **296–313 mph** within small sub-vortices, the National Weather Service officially rated the tornado an **EF3**. This was because the Enhanced Fujita scale is based strictly on ground-level structural damage, and the tornado remained mostly over open fields.

## The TWISTEX Tragedy

The most somber legacy of the El Reno tornado is the loss of the **TWISTEX** research team. The team, led by world-renowned engineer and storm researcher **Tim Samaras**, was caught by a rapid, "explosive" expansion of the tornado.

*   **Tim Samaras:** Founder of TWISTEX and a pioneer in tornado instrumentation.
*   **Paul Samaras:** Tim's son and the team's videographer.
*   **Carl Young:** A meteorologist and longtime chase partner.

The team's vehicle was caught by a violent sub-vortex and thrown nearly half a mile. These were the first recorded deaths of scientific researchers during a tornado pursuit, leading to a major shift in safety protocols within the chasing community.

## Total Impact

In total, the El Reno tornado claimed **8 lives**, including local residents and an amateur chaser. It served as a wake-up call regarding the dangers of "chaser convergence"—where hundreds of vehicles clog rural roads, making escape nearly impossible.
