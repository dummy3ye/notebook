In 1845, Sir John Franklin set sail from England with two of the most advanced ships of the Royal Navy: **HMS Erebus** and **HMS Terror**. Their mission was to find the "Holy Grail" of 19th-century navigation—the Northwest Passage. They were never seen alive again.

## The Disappearance

The expedition was equipped with three years' worth of supplies, including thousands of cans of preserved food. However, after entering the Canadian Arctic, the ships became trapped in the ice off King William Island. 

For nearly 170 years, the fate of the 129 men remained one of history's greatest mysteries. Search parties found scattered relics: a few graves, abandoned equipment, and harrowing evidence of lead poisoning, scurvy, and eventually, cannibalism as the men attempted to walk south across the frozen tundra.

## The Discoveries: 2014 and 2016

The mystery was finally solved through a combination of high-tech sonar and something that had been ignored for over a century: **Inuit oral history**.

*   **HMS Erebus (2014):** Discovered in relatively shallow water, the ship that once carried Franklin himself was found upright and well-preserved.
*   **HMS Terror (2016):** Found in Terror Bay, the ship was in even better condition, with glass panes still in the windows and desks with drawers full of papers and instruments.

## The Lessons of the Arctic

The discovery of the ships confirmed the accuracy of Inuit accounts, which had described a "great ship" sinking in specific locations. It also highlighted the hubris of the Victorian expedition, which relied on heavy equipment and Western technology while ignoring the survival techniques of the indigenous people who had thrived in the Arctic for millennia.

Today, the wrecks are part of the Wrecks of HMS Erebus and HMS Terror National Historic Site, co-managed by Parks Canada and the Inuit.
