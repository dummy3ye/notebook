On March 18, 1925, the United States witnessed what remains the deadliest and most record-breaking tornado in its history. Known as the **Tri-State Tornado**, it crossed through parts of Missouri, Illinois, and Indiana, leaving a path of destruction that changed the way the nation viewed severe weather.

## A Storm of Records

The Tri-State Tornado holds several records that have never been surpassed in the century since it occurred:
*   **Death Toll:** 695 people were killed, nearly twice as many as the next deadliest tornado in U.S. history.
*   **Path Length:** The tornado maintained a continuous track for **219 miles**.
*   **Duration:** It stayed on the ground for **3.5 hours**.
*   **Speed:** At its peak, the storm moved forward at a staggering **73 mph**.

## The Experience of the Storm

Unlike modern tornadoes that often have a distinct funnel shape, survivors described the Tri-State Tornado as an amorphous "wall of debris" or a "rolling black cloud" that hugged the ground. Because of its speed and unusual appearance, many people had no warning until the storm was upon them.

### Retrospective Rating
While the Fujita scale did not exist in 1925, modern meteorologists have retrospectively rated the Tri-State Tornado as an **F5**. It obliterated entire towns, most notably Murphysboro, Illinois, where 234 people lost their lives—the highest death toll for a single city in U.S. history.

## Legacy and the NWS

At the time, the term "tornado" was actually banned from weather forecasts to prevent panic. The 1925 tragedy proved that silence was more dangerous than the truth, eventually leading to the development of the modern warning systems and the National Weather Service we rely on today.
