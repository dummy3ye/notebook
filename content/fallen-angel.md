Alexandre Cabanel’s *The Fallen Angel* (1847) is one of the most iconic works of 19th-century French Academic art. Painted when Cabanel was just 24 years old, it famously blends technical perfection with intense Romantic emotion, portraying the biblical fall of Lucifer with unprecedented psychological depth.

## Analysis of the Gaze

Lucifer’s eyes are the focal point of the composition. He glares out from behind his crossed arms with a look of pure defiance and "wounded pride." A single, glistening tear is visible, humanizing the devil and suggesting a complex mix of regret, anger, and sorrow.

### The Idealized Body
Cabanel portrays Lucifer as a "Greek god" figure—muscular, youthful, and idealized. This beauty serves as a reminder of his former status as the "Morning Star," the most beautiful of all angels, making his descent into darkness even more tragic.

## Symbolism and Style

*   **The Wings:** His wings are dark and heavy, contrasting with the ethereal, light-filled angels seen flying in the distant background.
*   **The Background:** The contrast between the dark, rocky ground where Lucifer lies and the bright, airy heavens above emphasizes his isolation and the finality of his exile.

## Historical Reception

When Cabanel submitted this work to the Salon as a student in Rome, it shocked the judges. They were accustomed to "correct" religious depictions and found Cabanel’s portrayal of the Devil as a sympathetic, beautiful hero to be "too Romantic" and borderline scandalous. 

Today, it is celebrated as a masterpiece that captures the exact moment a choice was made: to reign in Hell rather than serve in Heaven.
