Katsushika Hokusai’s *The Great Wave off Kanagawa* (c. 1831) is perhaps the most famous work of Japanese art in the world. As the first print in his series *Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji*, it captures a moment of extreme tension between the fragile human element and the overwhelming force of nature.

## The Rogue Wave

The print depicts a massive wave—often mistaken for a tsunami, but scientifically identified as a "rogue wave"—threatening three fishing boats. The wave’s "claws" of foam seem to reach out for the sailors, emphasizing the predatory power of the sea.

### The Presence of Mount Fuji
In the background, Mount Fuji remains calm and stationary, its snowy peak providing a visual anchor. The mountain’s permanence stands in stark contrast to the chaotic, transient nature of the wave.

## Western Influence and Prussian Blue

While deeply rooted in the **Ukiyo-e** tradition, *The Great Wave* shows clear Western influences. Hokusai used linear perspective to create depth and, most significantly, a newly imported pigment known as **Prussian Blue**. This vibrant, synthetic dye allowed him to create a sense of depth and realism in the water that was previously impossible.

## A Global Icon

The image has become a global shorthand for the power of nature. It has influenced everything from the Impressionist painters of the 19th century to the "Great Wave" emoji used on smartphones today. It remains a masterpiece of composition, where every curve and line leads the eye back to the eternal silhouette of Mount Fuji.
